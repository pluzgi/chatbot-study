## 4.1 Sample and Descriptive Statistics

### Sample Flow and Exclusions

A total of N = 291 participants entered the study. Of these, 79 did not complete the post-task survey and were excluded from analysis. An additional 7 participants failed the attention check (incorrectly identifying the chatbot's topic) and were also excluded. No participants were missing condition assignment or donation decision data. This resulted in a final analytic sample of N = 205 (70.4% completion rate).

**Table 1**
*Sample Flow and Exclusions*

| Stage | N | Excluded |
|-------|---|----------|
| Initial participants | 291 | — |
| After incomplete survey exclusion | 212 | 79 |
| After failed attention check exclusion | 205 | 7 |
| Final analytic sample | 205 | — |

*Note.* Participant flow from initial recruitment to final analytic sample. Orange bars indicate exclusions due to incomplete surveys (n = 79) and failed attention checks (n = 7).

### Condition Assignment

Randomization resulted in approximately balanced cell sizes, with minor variation due to the online recruitment process: Condition A (n = 50, 24.4%), Condition B (n = 53, 25.9%), Condition C (n = 45, 22.0%), and Condition D (n = 57, 27.8%).

### Donation Rates

The overall donation rate was 91.7% (95% CI [87.1, 94.8]), substantially higher than anticipated. Donation rates were similarly high across all conditions, ranging from 90.0% to 93.3%. The narrow range (3.3 percentage points) between conditions suggests minimal differentiation due to experimental manipulations.

### Demographics

The sample was predominantly female (52.7%), with 44.9% male, 1.0% non-binary, and 1.5% preferring not to say. Age distribution was concentrated in the 35–44 bracket (38.0%), with lower representation at the extremes (18–24: 5.9%; 65+: 4.4%). German was the primary language for 81.0% of participants, followed by French (8.8%) and English (4.9%). Education levels were high, with 48.8% holding university degrees and an additional 16.6% with applied sciences degrees. A large majority (80.5%) reported Swiss voting eligibility.

### Risk Perception and Trust

Risk perception (OUT-RISK) was moderate across conditions (overall M = 3.12, SD = 1.46 on a 1–6 scale), with Condition A showing the lowest risk perception (M = 2.73) and Condition C the highest (M = 3.41). Trust levels (OUT-TRUST) were relatively high and consistent (overall M = 4.40, SD = 1.32), with Condition D showing slightly higher trust (M = 4.54).

### Dashboard Preferences (Conditions C and D)

Participants in high-control conditions (C and D) configured their data donation preferences using the Granular Consent Dashboard. Analysis includes only participants who chose to donate (C: n = 42; D: n = 53).

**Table 5**
*Dashboard Configuration Frequencies for Donors in Conditions C and D*

|-----------|--------|-------------------|-------------------|

| | Topics only | 7 (16.7%) | 5 (9.4%) |
| | Questions only | 4 (9.5%) | 6 (11.3%) |
| **Permitted Purpose** | Academic only | 31 (73.8%) | 32 (60.4%) |

*Note.* Percentages calculated within each condition among donors only.

The most common configuration in both conditions was full data scope with academic-only purpose, Swiss server storage, and retention until purpose is fulfilled. The majority (73.8% in C, 79.2% in D) opted to share full conversation data. Notably, 73.8% (C) and 60.4% (D) restricted use to academic purposes only, and 50.0% (C) and 41.5% (D) preferred Swiss server storage, suggesting data sovereignty concerns.
